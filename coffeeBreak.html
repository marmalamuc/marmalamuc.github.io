Espresso

[]

There are no such things as "espresso beans" or "espresso roast":

  • Espresso is a method of brewing, just like pour-over, French press, or drip.
  • There aren't specific beans for making espresso; there isn't a specific roast.
  • Any coffee beans can make espresso.

Pull a doppio with a Gaggia Classic

I buy light roast beans, which you generally brew at higher temperatures, close to boiling. A doppio (or “doubleshot”) is the standard shot that gives you 45ml or 1.5oz when pulled as a normale.

I follow the instructions from this Lance Hedrick video for the temp surfing & pre-infusion steps.
  1. Turn the Gaggia on, and allow it to heat for at least 20 minutes.
    Some coffee creators tell you to let it heat for 40 minutes to allow the boiler to cycle a couple times.
  2. Prepare your puck:
    1. Grind your beans into a dry portafilter.
    2. Rake the grounds with a WDT tool to break up any clumps.
      You can make one cheaply with cork & acupuncture needles, or just use an unwound paperclip.
    3. Tamp the grounds to level.
    4. Set the portafilter aside. Don’t (clock) it into the group head yet.
  3. Temperature surf to brew at a consistent temperature.
    Because the Gaggia doesn’t have a thermostat, you can’t be 100% sure what temperature your water will be when you start brewing. Temperature surfing manipulates the boiler’s heating cycles to narrow the temperature range when brewing. This method should give you water between 208–209°F at the boiler, and 200–201°F at the group head.
    1. When the (brew) button light is lit, flip the (brew) button and fill any heat-safe container until the light turns off.
      This light indicates that the machine is “ready to brew”.
    2. Wait until the (brew) light turns back on, then count 10 seconds.
    3. While waiting, wipe the group head with a dry towel.
    4. At 10 seconds, flip the (steam) button, then wait 5 seconds.
  4. Pre-infuse the puck:
    1. Affix the portafilter to the group head and place a shot glass underneath.
    2. After the (steam) button has been flipped for 5–8 seconds, turn it off.
      8 seconds should be your max pre-infusion time here; any longer, and you risk losing temperature at the group head.
    3. Place your heat-safe container under the wand.
    4. Immediately open the steam wand valve 1/8-to-1/4 turn, and flip the (brew) button.
      Opening the steam wand reduces the flow rate at the group head, soaking the puck without forcing water through it.
    5. Close the steam wand valve after 8 seconds, and continue brewing until you have 60ml/2oz.
      If you can fit a scale under your group head & portafilter, measure (60g) output. This is much more precise that measuring volume according to marks on a container.
  5. Let the container with hot water cool, then drink, water plants, pour back into the reservoir, or something else.

A note on Barista Parlor

Barista Parlor might have been the first Third Wave roaster in Nashville; certainly, they were the first nationally recognized name. You can get an excellent coffee beverage at one of their locations, but I don’t spend my money there anymore. For a couple reasons.

First, the baristas have always seemed a bit… snobby. Not all of them! To some extent, you get that kind of attitude with craft anything, but it has always felt more pronounced at BP than any of the places I recommend above. Talk to a coffee enthusiasts in Nashville or read some Google reviews and you’ll see what I mean.

Second, and most importantly, their labor practices have recently come under scrutiny. In short, they fired their longest tenured employee for trying to organize a union. Here are some articles that go into more detail:

  • WPLN (NPR affiliate)
  • Nashville Scene
  • WSMV (NBC affiliate)

Their response is predictable, boilerplate legalese. And it’s telling that they gave their employees a raise immediately after firing Xochitl. To be fair, BP employees’ compensation package does offer 401(k), health benefits, & PTO, which is unusual for the food service industry (but should be the baseline for any & all employment).

But I can’t possibly take seriously the idea that a “bad attitude” constitutes cause after seven years of employment. For me, there are way too many great coffee places in town to continue supporting BP.
